Here's how to gauge the project even w/o them stating their vision clearly, based on their plans, and figure out if they have any sustainable utility.

3/The general principle I use when evaluating roadmap items is that frequently, only a few are actually sustainable value, and most are just noise. At best, they are marketing/hype building. At worst, they are ponzi redistribution mechanics or even evidence of stealing.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

4/That being said, it is worth saying that most of those roadmap items can be totally valid in the right context. Usually when I see them I discount them, but they can all serve a purpose. Most are amber rather than red flags.

5/A bunch of amber flags with a bunch of green flags is fine. The problem is when it's all amber flags, or all amber and red flags. So, without further ado, let's have the first sub-thread...

Roadmap Analysis 1: Marketing, whitelisting, hype, Discord, billboards, updated website, etc.

A common category of items in roadmaps is basically marketing, but some stuff is really purely about directly building excitement.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

2/There's nothing wrong with marketing. In fact, an NFT project with no marketing is dead in the water.

However, marketing does not create sustainable value for the project. It's just a way to attract more investors.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

3/Yes, sure, "building a brand" and all that, but as per my review, even BAYC, "the ultimate brand", has concrete utility ( https://t.co/0RgWfuWGbu ) - and likely were delivering on that long before they became the Apple of the NFT space.

4/So when I see anything that's around building hype, setting up or ramping up Discord, advertising, doing stuff on the website... I just ignore it.

Doing a good marketing job is table stakes for NFT projects. It's not something worth stating on the roadmap imho.

Item 1, done.

Roadmap Analysis 2: Rewards, giveaways, raffles, cash prizes, lambos, scavenger hunts...

Spoiler: I tend to discount those too. They are a special crypto/nft-popular kind of marketing. Imho they don't make a whole lot of sense.

2/Why? Because they are actually draining the financial resources of the project to give them right back to the investors. If the project is actually building something, it should be using the money it raised to build that thing. I elaborated on this here: https://t.co/FBRpRSbXyb

Roadmap Analysis 3: Floor sweeps

These still appear surprisingly frequently in roadmaps. I consider them a generally red-ish amber flag.

Floor sweeps add absolutely nothing to the project. Not only that, but they largely don't work.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

2/As an investor, of course, everyone loves the idea that "the project" will come and help the floor move up. But it's a bit like the Baron Munchausen pulling himself out of the swamp by his own bootstraps.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

3/Investors gave the project money to do <something> and the best thing that the project can think to do is to buy back the NFTs from the investors.

I mean, I see how it might seem appealing as a tactic, even to a sensible project, but it's appallingly self-referential.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

4/#LazyLions have had, for as long as I was there, the "Roars>Floors" meme to convey that a successful project focuses on something other than the floor (though ironically they did conduct a floor sweep at least once).

But at least they didn't have it on the god damned roadmap.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

5/To put the floor sweep ON THE ROADMAP seems... bizarre. Like, "we know that a bunch of y'all investors are going to see the project is worthless so we'll use your own money to try and cover that up".

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

6/And yet it happens. I've seen it.

And when I see it, I add a mental red flag, and I think you should too, if you want to invest in projects with a long-term, sustainable outlook.

Roadmap Analysis 5: Some kind of $TOKEN

Let's finish with one that's all the rage right now. "We're going to release our own token", usually with some kind of funny name.

Urgh.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

2/Tokens, which are one of the fundamental building blocks of web3, are awesome. I 100% support the tokenisation of most things.

BUT YOU HAVE TO HAVE A REAL-WORLD PURPOSE FOR THAT TOKEN

Otherwise it's just trash.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

3/Now, for some projects a token might well make sense. But if the roadmap lists a token without really carefully explaining how it makes sense, I consider it a likely sign that the founders are in to make some money.

How can a token make sense?

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

4/The key questions I ask myself to decide if a token makes sense is:

Why would anyone outside the project ascribe any value to this token?

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

5/The key is that interface between the world of the project, and the rest of the world. If there is no reason for someone outside the project to buy the token, then, functionally, it is just another way to redistribute funds inside the project.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

6/Now, I'll carve out an exception here, which is where the token is really just for internal use to help facilitate some kind of internal market, for access to some sort of scarce resource inside the project.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

7/For example, if Vitalik started a project and "asking questions from Vitalik" was a scarce resource, and there's a $VIT token that you accrue and you can ask a question from Vitalik (whose time is scarce) by spending the token... ok. That can be sensible.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

8/But importantly, even if that's true, the token will be worthless to anyone outside the project unless it has value to those outside the project.

So in such a case, it's not a red flag, but it's still not something that adds long term sustainable value.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 24, 2021

9/In short, in most cases, if there's mention of a token, it's probably a red flag, maybe an amber flag in some exceptional cases.

Enough for today - the thread of thread will continue tomorrow!

Remember to follow and RT and all that :-)

Roadmap Analysis 6: Staking

Oh boy.

As far as I can tell, staking has noble origins. The purpose of staking in crypto is to enable proof-of-stake algorithms, which are much more environmentally friendly than proof-of-work.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

2/Of course, that's not at all happening in NFT projects. There is no proof algorithm in your average NFT project that benefits from staking (if there is one, please let me know in the responses!).

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

3/Instead, staking in NFT projects exists for one purpose and one purpose only: to stop people from listing their NFTs, therefore reducing the supply available for purchase, therefore driving the price up.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

4/In my thread about NFT-powered clubs ( https://t.co/kzuL1oQpmE ) I mentioned the inexorable arithmetic that will make good clubs go up in value over time.

Staking is simply an artificial attempt to force that to happen.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

5/Often the reward for the staking is a token (see the previous instalment for my opinion of that - https://t.co/b8OpFdifwo ). Usually it has some kind of worth that will be defined later (i.e. no worth). Or maybe it becomes part of another internal, redistributive game.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

6/Now I'm not saying that staking never has a place in any project. I can imagine some kind of vision where it makes sense. For example, where the NFT maps to certain rw resources and a longer-term commitment of those resources is needed. Staking could model that.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

7/But in most projects, that's not the case. And even then, in my experience it's rather tenuous (if the resources are already detached from the NFT, what does it matter if it's staked?).

So, staking is an amber flag. Useless, no sustainable value-add, pure floor focus.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

Roadmap Analysis 7: Community funds

This one is a bit more ambiguous. In some cases, it can be evidence of outright stealing. In other cases, it's just a bit of weirdness unlikely to add much to the project.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

2/Let's deal with the thievery first.

I've argued before, and firmly believe, that money raised by a project during mint belongs to the project, not the founders ( https://t.co/qdJ6vye7Om ), in the same way that money raised by a startup belongs to the company.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

3/In some cases, it appears that the founders consider the mint money their payday. They walk off with hundreds of Eth, and then are oh so generous to leave some small amount in the "community wallet" so the project can, you know, pay for something some day.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

4/If you do that by mistake, because you didn't understand what an NFT project was... ok, maybe I'll give you a pass. If you do that after reading this thread, sorry but you're a fraudster.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

5/As an investor, I would strongly encourage you to demand a clear answer from the project as to where the mint money is going. Some amount paid to founders is reasonable. If the lion's share goes to them, it's not.

So, back to community wallet.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

6/If the "community wallet" is what's left of the mint after the founders have robbed the project, a pittance left behind to be able to pretend that the project has means to do things... that's a red flag and imho you should run away from that project.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

7/It's also a red flag that can be applied retroactively. For projects you're in right now, that you invested a while ago... is the team willing to provide any transparency over where the money is going, or has gone?

Are they hiding a lambo or two, bought with project funds?

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

8/It can be an amber flag if the team has, reasonably, split the pot into "dev funds" and "community funds", where most funds are controlled by the team to build the project, and a portion are given to the community to be more DAO-like, give them a sense of owning something.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

9/I still consider that amber and discard it when I see it on a roadmap, though - because unless the purpose of the project is collective investment, it's a form of giving the funds back to the community and saying "we don't know what to do with all this money lol". pic.twitter.com/DHqmn7HpvW

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

10/The only context where I'd see a "community wallet" as a green flag is if the purpose of the project is explicitly "collective investment" - i.e., pooling resources together to buy stuff no single investor could afford and share the benefits.

Otherwise, ignore this.

Roadmap Analysis 8: Airdrops, companions, serums, etc

So, those are tricky, because they can have value and have proven, e.g. in BAYC's case, to have enormous value.

The key is context and timing.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

2/Here it all depends on what the project is intending to achieve.

Airdrops/companions serve a few valid purposes:
- fun (in an artist launchpad)
- bring in more members (in a club)
- raise more funds (any project that needs it)

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

3/In a project like @robotosNFT, the first two items were the primary reason for the robopets. It enabled new fans to join the project at a lower price point, and they were used in fun internal games, part of the artist experience Pablo invited people into.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

4/In BAYC, the primary reason for the MAYC was, I believe, to expand the size of the club. Since at that point BAYCs were way out of the price range of their ideal members, it made a lot of sense.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

5/But notice that in both those cases, the project was successful *before* it did an airdrop or companion mint. The additional NFT was integrated into an already successful, relatively mature project. And that's the key point that makes those useless on roadmaps.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

6/If your project is already successful and you drop some kind of companion for <reason that makes sense>, great.

But if your project is not successful, a companion will not get you there. Quite the opposite.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

7/If you're in a club, and the proposition of that club is poorly defined, it's unclear who the ideal members are, and people are leaving the ship already... bringing in an extra few thousand people who also don't know what they're buying won't solve the club's problems.

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021

8/So in short, airdrops and companions make no sense on roadmaps.

OF COURSE, if it makes sense, the project will drop a companion.

But until then, it's zero value add to anyone - except the team who maybe get to do another cash grab (if it's a paid companion).

— Daniel Tenner (swombat.eth) (@swombat) November 25, 2021
